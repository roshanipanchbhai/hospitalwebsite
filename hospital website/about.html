History:

Medicare is a federal health insurance program for people aged 65 and older, people with certain disabilities, and people with End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD). Medicare was created in 1965 by Title XVIII of the Social Security Act.

The Medicare Hospital Insurance (HI) program, also known as Part A, provides coverage for inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility stays, home health care, and hospice care. Part A is funded by payroll taxes paid by most workers and their employers.

The Medicare Supplemental Insurance (Medigap) program, also known as Part B, provides coverage for services that are not covered by Part A, such as doctor visits, outpatient care, and prescription drugs. Part B is voluntary and is purchased by most Medicare beneficiaries.

The Medicare Advantage (MA) program, also known as Part C, is a private health insurance plan that provides all of the benefits of Part A and Part B. MA plans are offered by private insurance companies and are regulated by the federal government.

Medicare has had a significant impact on the health care system in the United States. It has helped to reduce the number of uninsured people, and it has helped to control the cost of health care. Medicare is also credited with improving the quality of care for seniors.
